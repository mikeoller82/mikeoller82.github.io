Where ConvertKit Actually Wins (And Where It Embarrasses Itself)

The Plain-Text Focus Is a Feature, Not a Bug

Every other email platform is trying to make your emails look like a landing page. Kit goes the opposite direction. Plain text. No images unless you specifically add them. No fancy layouts. The Pricing Elephant in the Room

Let's talk about what everyone's thinking: Kit is expensive. At $39/month for 1,000 subscribers (Creator plan), you're paying almost $0.04 per subscriber per month just for basic features. The Creator Pro plan starts at $79/month and adds subscriber scoring and advanced reporting.

Here's the thing though: pricing is relative to results. If you're sending emails that nobody opens, the cheapest platform in the world is still too expensive. Pricing Tiers That Actually Make Sense

  1. Free (Newsletter): 0-10,000 subscribers, unlimited emails, basic automation, digital product sales (3.5% + $0.30 fee). Requires Kit branding and Recommendations feature.
  2. Creator: Starts at $39/month (1,000 subscribers), scales to $199/month (25,000 subscribers). Includes live chat support, advanced automation, integrations.
  3. Creator Pro: Starts at $79/month (1,000 subscribers), scales to $279/month (25,000 subscribers). Adds subscriber scoring, Facebook custom audiences, newsletter referral system.

Who Should NOT Use ConvertKit

This is the part other reviews skip. ConvertKit is wrong for you if:

  • You need beautiful, designed emails. Kit's strength is plain-text. If your brand relies on visual email marketing, look at Klaviyo or Mailchimp.
  • You're on a tight budget with a small list. Free plan is fine, but once you hit 1,000 subscribers, you're paying $39/month minimum.
  • You need advanced CRM features. Kit is an email platform, not a CRM. For complex sales pipelines, use ActiveCampaign or HubSpot.
  • You sell physical products. Kit's monetization tools are built for digital products and subscriptions. For e-commerce, you want Klaviyo's Shopify integration.
