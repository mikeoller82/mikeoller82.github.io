Mailchimp Is Charging You for Ghosts — And Kit Isn't

Here's a thing Mailchimp doesn't advertise: if the same person signs up for two of your lists, you get charged twice. Two lists, one human, two billable subscribers. Multiply that by automations, segments, and lead magnets that create duplicate entries, and a meaningful chunk of your "subscriber count" is phantom billing.

Kit (formerly ConvertKit) counts unique subscribers. One person, one charge. Doesn't matter if they're tagged in 14 different segments, signed up through three forms, and triggered six automations. One human. One line item. That single architectural decision saves creators hundreds of dollars per year compared to Mailchimp, AWeber, and most legacy ESPs.

But that's not the only reason 500,000+ creators migrated to Kit. Let's talk about what it actually does well, where it falls apart, and whether it's still worth using after the September 2025 price hike.

Kit's free Newsletter plan gives you up to 10,000 subscribers. Mailchimp's free plan caps you at 500. That's not a typo. Twenty times more subscribers at zero cost.

On Kit's free tier, you get:

  • Unlimited landing pages and forms — embed modals, sticky bars, slide-ins, inline forms. All of them.
  • Unlimited broadcasts — send as many newsletters as you want to your full list.
  • Creator Profile — a mini website that hosts your newsletter content, links, and products.
  • Opt-in incentive delivery — automatically deliver freebie files when someone subscribes.

If you're a blogger, YouTuber, or podcaster with fewer than 10,000 subscribers who just needs to send weekly emails and capture leads, you will never pay a cent. Compare that to Mailchimp, where you're paying $26/month once you cross 500 subscribers.

The gap gets worse as you grow. At 5,000 subscribers:

  • Mailchimp Standard: $100/month
  • Kit Creator: $33/month

The Automation Builder That Makes Everyone Else Look Dated

This is Kit's actual moat. Not the pricing. Not the free plan. The visual automation builder.

Most email platforms give you a flowchart that looks like it was designed in 2009 — boxes connected by lines, with conditions buried in modal windows. Kit's automation builder is clean, visual, and genuinely intuitive. You can build sequences like:

  1. New subscriber signs up through your lead magnet form
  2. Wait 1 day → send welcome email with freebie link
  3. Wait 3 days → send educational email #1
  4. Did they click the link? → Tag as "engaged" and move to sales sequence
  5. Did they ignore it? → Send a different angle email
  6. After 14 days with no engagement → Move to re-engagement sequence

You build this visually, drag-and-drop, with conditions, triggers, and actions at every step. It's not the most powerful automation engine on the market — ActiveCampaign wins that fight — but it's the most accessible one that doesn't require a certification course to use.

Where Kit Will Drive You Crazy

I'm not going to pretend this platform is flawless. There are real frustrations:

  • A/B testing is embarrassingly limited — you can only test subject lines. Two variations. That's it. No sender name testing. No content testing. No timing testing. If you're used to Mailchimp's multivariate testing, Kit feels like bringing a butter knife to a sword fight.
  • Design options are basic — the email templates are minimal. If you want polished, image-heavy newsletters with complex layouts, Kit isn't built for that. It's designed for text-focused creators, and it shows. Custom CSS or HTML is required for anything beyond the basics.
  • Reporting is thin — open rates, click rates, and unsubscribes. That's mostly it. If you need heat maps, geo-tracking, device breakdowns, or revenue attribution, you'll be disappointed.
  • Pricing jumps are steep — the September 2025 price increase hit hard. Going from free to $33/month when you cross 10,000 subscribers is a cliff, not a ramp. And the Pro plan at $66/month adds features (Facebook custom audiences, referral system) that feel like they should be in Creator.
  • Ecommerce integrations are weak — compared to ActiveCampaign or Klaviyo, Kit's ecommerce story is thin. If your primary revenue is physical product sales, look elsewhere.

Kit vs Mailchimp: The Real Comparison

Let's stop pretending these are the same product for the same audience.

Kit wins on:

  • Subscriber counting — unique subscribers only. Mailchimp counts duplicates across lists.
  • Free plan generosity — 10,000 vs 500 subscribers. Not even close.
  • Automation simplicity — visual builder that creators can actually use without training.
  • Creator-first features — paid newsletters, digital product sales, creator network recommendations.
  • Deliverability — multiple independent tests confirm Kit's inbox placement rates are consistently strong.

Mailchimp wins on:

  • Design and templates — far more polished email templates and a better drag-and-drop editor.
  • A/B testing — multivariate testing across subject lines, content, send time, and sender name.
  • Ecommerce integrations — deeper Shopify, WooCommerce, and BigCommerce connections.
  • Reporting and analytics — more granular data, better visualizations, comparative benchmarks.
  • Integrations ecosystem — more native integrations overall.

If you're a creator selling courses, coaching, or digital products, Kit is the obvious choice. If you're an ecommerce brand that needs sophisticated segmentation, product recommendation emails, and detailed purchase behavior tracking, Mailchimp or Klaviyo is the better fit.

Who Should Use Kit (And Who Shouldn't)

Kit is built for:

  • Bloggers and content creators who want simple, reliable email delivery
  • Course creators and coaches who sell through email sequences
  • Newsletter writers who prioritize text content over visual design
  • Anyone with under 10,000 subscribers who wants to stop paying for email entirely
  • Creators migrating from Mailchimp who are tired of paying for duplicate subscribers

Look elsewhere if:

  • You're an ecommerce brand that needs purchase behavior segmentation and product emails
  • You care deeply about email design — Kit's templates are functional, not beautiful
  • You need serious A/B testing across multiple variables
  • Your list is 50,000+ and you need enterprise-grade reporting and support
