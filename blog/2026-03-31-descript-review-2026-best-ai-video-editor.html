The Editing Time Breakdown: Where the Hours Actually Go

Before Descript, my podcast editing workflow looked like this:

  1. Import and sync — 10 minutes per episode
  2. Remove filler words manually — 45-60 minutes. Every "um," "uh," "like" found and cut by scrubbing through the timeline
  3. Cut tangents and dead air — 30-45 minutes. Finding the moments, marking in-out points, making the cuts
  4. Audio cleanup — 20 minutes. Noise reduction, level normalization, EQ adjustments in a separate tool
  5. Export and review — 15 minutes. Export, listen back, fix any cuts that sounded unnatural, re-export
  6. Create show notes and timestamps — 30 minutes. Listen again, write timestamps, pull quotes
  7. Generate captions — 20 minutes. Transcribe manually or run through a separate captioning tool

Total: 170-200 minutes per episode. Call it 3 hours average.

After Descript: The Same Episode in 47 Minutes

Here's what the workflow looks like now:

  1. Import — 2 minutes. Drag files in, Descript transcribes automatically.
  2. Filler word removal — 2 minutes. Click one button. Review the highlights. Bulk-approve. The 45-minute task that defined my editing life is now a 2-minute checkbox.
  3. Cut tangents and dead air — 15 minutes. Highlight paragraphs in the transcript, delete. The video cuts correspondingly. No timeline scrubbing.
  4. Audio cleanup (Studio Sound) — 1 minute. Toggle on. Done. It handles noise, echo, and level balancing automatically.
  5. Export — 3 minutes. One export, no separate audio tool needed.
  6. Show notes — 10 minutes. The transcript is already there. I highlight key segments and copy-paste into my show notes template.
  7. Captions — 0 minutes. Auto-generated. Export as SRT. Done.
  8. Buffer for random edits — 14 minutes. There's always something — a mispronunciation, a segment that needs re-recording, a last-minute change.

Total: 47 minutes. That's a 74% reduction in editing time per episode.

Where the ROI Story Falls Apart

I've been mostly positive. Here's where Descript doesn't deliver the time savings it promises:

  • Episodes over 60 minutes — Descript's reliability degrades on longer recordings. Lag, crashes, and performance issues are documented by users on r/podcasting. My 45-minute episodes run fine. My occasional 90-minute episodes are painful.
  • Non-talk content — if your content is primarily B-roll, music videos, or cinematic footage, Descript's text-based editing doesn't apply. The time savings are zero.
  • Complex edits — multi-cam sequences, advanced color grading, motion graphics. Descript can't do these. You still need Premiere or DaVinci for anything beyond basic editing.
  • Transcription errors — industry jargon, accented speech, and technical terms get mis-transcribed. Every error requires manual correction before you can edit efficiently.
  • Learning curve — Descript markets itself as "editing as easy as a document." It's not. Concepts like Compositions, Scenes, and Sequences take 2-3 weeks to learn. My 47-minute workflow took 6 weeks to develop.

The Time Savings by Content Type

Not all content benefits equally from Descript. Here's my actual data:

  • Podcast episodes (45 min): 74% time savings. From 3 hours to 47 minutes. Maximum ROI.
  • Talking-head YouTube (10 min): 65% time savings. From 45 minutes to 16 minutes.
  • Interview-based content (30 min): 70% time savings. From 2 hours to 36 minutes.

The pattern is clear: the more your content is people talking, the more Descript saves you. The more it's visual storytelling, the less it matters.
