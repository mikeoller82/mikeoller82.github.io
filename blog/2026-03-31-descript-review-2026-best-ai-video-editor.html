Here's the honest verdict after two years of daily use. ## What Descript Actually Does (Without the Marketing Fog) Descript isn't a traditional video editor with AI bolted on. It's a text-based editing tool that treats your video transcript as the primary interface. You edit words on a screen, and the video changes underneath. Delete a sentence from the transcript, and the corresponding video clip vanishes. Rearrange paragraphs, and the footage reshuffles with them. That core mechanic is still brilliant in 2026. It's the reason podcast editors, solo creators, and marketing teams keep gravitating toward it. You don't need to learn timeline editing, keyframe animation, or any of the nonsense that makes Premiere Pro feel like piloting a spacecraft. But Descript has grown well beyond "edit video by editing text." A 10-minute YouTube video that takes 90 minutes to cut in Premiere takes me roughly 35 minutes in Descript. Not because Descript is faster at everything — it's not — but because the workflow matches how my brain works. I read the transcript, delete the dead air, move a section that should be earlier, and I'm done. For talking-head content, interview footage, or anything where the audio is the primary driver, text-based editing crushes traditional timelines. I've watched clients who've never touched video software edit their own testimonials in 20 minutes flat. The limitation? Anything that requires precise visual timing — motion graphics, B-roll synchronization, color grading — still needs a proper NLE. Descript is not Premiere, and pretending it is would be dishonest. ### Studio Sound Deserves More Credit This is the feature I use on every single project. Studio Sound takes noisy audio — room echo, fan noise, mic hum — and cleans it up with a single toggle. I've tested it against iZotope RX, and for casual use, Descript holds up. It's not surgical enough for broadcast audio, but for YouTube and podcasts? It's genuinely impressive. I recorded an entire podcast episode in a coffee shop last month. Studio Sound made it sound like I was in a treated studio. That alone justified the subscription for me. ### Filler Word Removal — Use With Caution One-click filler word removal sounds amazing until you realize it can make you sound robotic. Descript finds every "um" and "uh" and lets you bulk-delete them. The problem is that natural speech includes those pauses for a reason. Remove all of them, and you sound like you're reading a teleprompter. My rule: delete filler words only when they're excessive. A couple of "ums" in a 15-minute video is human. Fifteen of them is distracting. ## Where Descript Falls Short ### Overdub Is Impressive But Limited Overdub lets you type new words and hear them in your cloned voice. The technology is remarkable — I've used it to fix mispronounced names without re-recording. But it struggles with emotional range. If you whispered a line or shouted with excitement, Overdub will deliver it in a flat, neutral tone. It's useful for corrections, not for creative direction. There are also ethical guardrails. Descript requires you to record a consent script proving it's your voice, and Overdub only works on your own vocal profile. You can't clone someone else's voice, which is exactly the right call. ### The Free Tier Is Basically a Demo Descript's free plan gives you 1 hour of transcription per month and watermarked exports. That's enough to test the interface but not enough to produce anything real. You'll hit the paywall within your first project. ### Export Quality Has Limits Descript maxes out at 4K export, which sounds fine until you need specific codec settings. You can't fine-tune bitrate, choose between H.264 and H.265, or set custom frame rates beyond the basics. If you're producing content for broadcast or need archival-quality masters, you'll still export to Premiere or DaVinci Resolve for final delivery. ## Descript vs. HeyGen: Different Tools for Different Jobs These two get compared constantly, but they solve different problems. **HeyGen** is an AI avatar video generator. You type a script, pick an AI presenter, and it generates a video of that person speaking your words. It's built for scale — pumping out dozens of product explainers, training videos, or multilingual content without cameras, lights, or talent. **Descript** is for editing existing footage. You record yourself, then use Descript to clean it up, cut it down, and polish it for publication. If you need to create videos without being on camera, HeyGen is the move. If you're already recording yourself and want to edit faster, Descript wins. They're not really competitors — they're complementary tools in a modern video stack.
